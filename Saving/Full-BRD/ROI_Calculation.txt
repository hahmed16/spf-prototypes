Savings Scheme (Provident Fund) 

Interest Crediting Mechanism 

Part A — Interest Crediting Mechanism 

This section sets out the mechanism for crediting the annual return on investment (ROI) to 
individual member accounts. The approach is based on simple interest, applied monthly, 
with no intra-year compounding. 

A.1 General Rule 

Interest on any transaction — whether a deposit or a withdrawal — begins accruing from the 
1st of the month following the transaction date. Deposits increase the interest-bearing 
balance; withdrawals reduce it by the same mechanism. The opening balance for the year 
accrues interest for all 12 months. Interest is calculated monthly on a simple basis (annual 
rate ÷ 12) applied to the outstanding balance, and the total accrued return is credited to the 
account at year-end. 

The monthly interest rate is derived from the annual rate as follows: 

Monthly rate = Annual ROI ÷ 12 

For example, if the approved ROI for the year is 3%, the monthly rate is 0.25%.  With the 
simple interest approach, this 0.25% is not compounded, but multiplied by the number of 
months.  

In all case, your ROI should be set in a way that that the end of the year, the sum of the 
individual accounts equal to the total value of assets. 

A.2 Year-End Rollover 

At the end of each year, the account balance — comprising the opening balance, net 
contributions and withdrawals during the year, and the total interest credited for the year — 
becomes the opening balance for the following year.  

A.3 Crediting Rate for Mid-Year Withdrawals 

The final crediting rate for a given year is determined by the Board only at year-end, once 
actual investment performance is known. An employee who withdraws part or all of their 
balance during the calendar year cannot benefit from a rate that has not yet been set. 
Accordingly, any full withdrawal processed during the year is credited at the guaranteed 
minimum rate of 2% up to the date of withdrawal (calculated using the monthly simple 
interest method described in A.1). The employee does not receive any retrospective 
adjustment if the final crediting rate for the year turns out to be higher than 2%. 

A.4 Application to Specific Cases 

The following examples assume an approved ROI of 3% for the year 2026. 

Case 1: Monthly Contributions on a Fixed Date 

If an employee contributes OMR 100 on the 1st of each month from January to December 
(total: OMR 1,200), interest on each contribution starts from the 1st of the following month. 
The January contribution earns interest for 11 months, the February contribution for 10 
months, and so on. The December contribution earns no interest that year. 

Year-end accumulated value: 

100 × (1 + 3% × 11/12) + 100 × (1 + 3% × 10/12) + … + 100 × (1 + 3% × 0/12) = OMR 
1,216.50 

Total interest earned: OMR 16.50. 

Case 2: Monthly Contributions on Variable Dates 

If contributions are made on different dates within each month (e.g. 15 January, 4 February, 
23 March), the crediting rule is unchanged: interest starts from the 1st of the month following 
the contribution. The exact date within the month is irrelevant. The calculation is therefore 
identical to Case 1, provided the same months are involved. 

Case 3: Lump-Sum Contribution at Year-End 

If an employee deposits OMR 1,200 on 25 December 2026, the contribution falls in 
December and interest begins from 1 January 2027. No interest is credited for 2026. 

1,200 × (1 + 3% × 0/12) = OMR 1,200.00 

The amount rolls into the 2027 opening balance. 

Case 4: Partial Withdrawals 

Withdrawals are treated as negative contributions. If an employee with an opening balance 
of OMR 5,000 withdraws OMR 500 per month from February through May (total: OMR 
2,000), the opening balance earns a full year of interest, and each withdrawal reduces the 
interest-bearing balance from the 1st of the following month: 

Component Calculation Amount (OMR) 

Opening balance 5,000 × 3% × 12/12 150.00 

Feb withdrawal −500 × 3% × 10/12 −12.50 

Mar withdrawal −500 × 3% × 9/12 −11.25 

Apr withdrawal −500 × 3% × 8/12 −10.00 

May withdrawal −500 × 3% × 7/12 −8.75 

Total interest  107.50 

 

The year-end account balance would be: 5,000 − 2,000 + 107.50 = OMR 3,107.50. 

A.5 Alternatives 

Instead of having the interest on the contributions in a given month begins accruing from the 
1st of the month following the transaction date, SPF could decide to have the interest begins 
accruing from the 15th of the month during which the transaction is made. The rationale for 

this approach is that on average transactions occur in the middle of the month.  The 
numerator would then become 11.5, 10.5, ....0.5 

Another more complex alternative would be accruing interest from the date of the transaction 
using the exact number of days between the transaction date and the 31 December.  Instead 
of dividing the ROI by 12, it would be divided by 365 (366 in bissextile years). 

As mentioned in section A.1, whatever the method that is selected, the ROI will have to be 
determined in such a way that the sum of individual accounts at the end of the year is equal 
to the market value of assets of the Provident Fund.
